The Holy Month of Ramadan: Iftar Recommendations from a Nutritionist

Ramadan fasting is considered a religious form of fasting resembling the infamous Intermittent Fasting regimen (Madkour et al. 2023; Patterson & Sears 2017).

Adult Muslims, more than 1.5 billion worldwide, practice this type of intermittent fasting as a form of religious tradition each year (Obaideen et al. 2022).

What is so interesting about this religious tradition is that this type of fasting (12-18 hours a day) offers a scientifically proven myriad of benefits nutritionally, physiologically, and metabolically speaking.

This includes but is not limited to:

  • improvement in weight status (Jahrami et al. 2020; Faris et al. 2020)

  • enhancement in metabolic and inflammatory markers (Faris et al. 2019 & Faris et al. 2012)

  • reduction in heart disease risk factors in healthy people (Jahrami et al. 2022; Madkour et al. 2023), and many more.

Although there are generally 2 main meals during a basic Ramadan day, “Iftar” and “Suhoor”, the main focus of this blog will be on the “Iftar” meal where 60% of the calories of the day are consumed (Shadman et al. 2015).

However, the long fasting hours are followed by the religious-social event of breaking the fast termed “Iftar” where high-in-calorie meals and sugary drinks tend to be readily consumed each night of the holy month of Ramadan.

This blog is all about guiding you on how to retain the benefits of the Ramadan fast this year, and to point out what you can do better at the evening feast from a nutritional perspective.
